What the Capstone project taught me about engineering.

My Journey

Reflecting back on the last 8 months...

I discovered that engineering design is iterative, constrained, and messy, not a tidy march from idea to prototype. Beyond calculations, the work meant framing the problem, checking assumptions, redefining scope, and weighing tradeoffs under real safety, cost, cleanliness, and integration limits. Midway through our capstone, changing client needs forced us to abandon our first solution and pivot—proof that requirements evolve and adaptability isn’t optional in design practice.

Accordingly, the attributes that matter most include diligence, resilience, technical competence, and clear communication. Sound analysis enables data‑driven choices, while communication and collaboration turn ideas into coordinated action. Engineers face setbacks and surprises, so bouncing back with accuracy is essential. Just as important is shared motivation: believing in the mission sustained our team, especially because our product aimed to enhance human healthcare, giving purpose to late nights and tough iterations.

This experience clarified engineers’ duty to the public. We are accountable to clients, teammates, operators, and ultimately the people touched by our systems. Our design choices affected worker exposure, operational downtime, and healthcare delivery; in our case, patients depended on reliable isotope production. That responsibility shaped every decision and underscored why engineers become de facto leaders—they must navigate risk, systems interactions, and human impacts while making timely calls that keep people safe and services running.

Looking ahead, I know one of my strengths is persistence during complex problem-solving. I also bring proficiency in CAD modelling, prototyping, designing to manufacture (DFM), and project management. I am proud of the work I did in this project including designing the hose coupler and assisting in the development of the test setup. At the same time, I want to keep developing my confidence in technical communication and decision-making under uncertainty. I plan to do that by seeking feedback more deliberately, documenting my reasoning more clearly, and continuing to take on projects that challenge both my technical and professional skills.
